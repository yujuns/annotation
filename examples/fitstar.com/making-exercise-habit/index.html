Making Exercise a Habit

By Judi Brown — FitStar Contributor

Brand new year, brand new start. This is the year you’re going to turn it all around and make regular exercise a habit. You know what you’re supposed to do to get healthy: work out regularly, eat healthy foods, drink more water, get more rest. Yet, we still find ourselves struggling to make any changes stick. How do we make having a fit, healthy lifestyle a habit?

The reality is, habits are easier to make than they are to break. If you repeat a behavior often enough, those synaptic pathways are going to get worn in. The human brain is a very adaptive piece of machinery.

Exercise wasn’t always an ingrained habit for me. I have a girlfriend from high school who still teases me about how “Judi, the personal trainer-exercise-queen” dreaded running the obligatory mile run in P.E. I would complete it (barely) kicking and screaming and complaining under my breath the entire time. I was slow, and sometimes I walked. I really had an aversion to running. I told myself that I didn’t have a runner’s body and that running just wasn’t for me.

But something shifted in college: a friend asked me to join her to run a charity 5K to raise money for breast cancer awareness, and since it was a charity close to my heart, I considered it. My mom passed away from breast cancer when I was 12, and I felt this could be an awesome race to run in her honor. But frankly, I really didn’t believe I could complete it. I thought maybe I’d try to start slowly and see how it went, but was far from convinced I could actually run a 5K. I bought a “Runner’s World” magazine, and I made a decision, that I was at least going to give it a shot. That was the beginning of my regular exercise habit.

I started running super small distances and built up my mileage very, very slowly. Every other morning I would throw my running shoes on and out the door I went. I did end up finishing the 5K and was overjoyed and slightly amazed that I had run the entire way. I felt strong, empowered, and fairly shocked that I could actually run the entire 3.1-mile distance. The girl who loathed running ran three-plus miles without stopping! I did it, and I was hooked. Cut to 2014, and seven full marathons later, and let’s just say my habit stuck. Today, long distance running has dropped down on the list of my favorite ways to exercise, but it was the true impetus for my fit and healthy lifestyle.

An excerpt from the book “The Power of Habit” by Charles Duhigg states:

“If you believe you can change – if you make it a habit – the change becomes real. This is the real power of habit: the insight that your habits are what you chose them to be. Once that choice occurs – and becomes automatic – it’s not only real, it starts to seem inevitable, the thing…that bears us irresistibly toward our destiny, whatever the latter may be.”

You are 100% in control of every decision you can make. The key is to train yourself to identify the habits you’re hoping to form. With that in mind, here are some steps you can take to increase your chances of success in the endeavor:

Write it down, document it, and track your progress
Write down the habit you want to change, and write down specific plans for achieving that goal. Rather than writing “I will exercise,” write, “I will start exercising 30 minutes a day, 3 times a week, on Monday, Thursday, and Saturday.” Get specific with your exercise habit.

One small step at a time
Try only changing one habit at a time. Instead of “I’m going to quit eating junk food, start exercising with FitStar, go to sleep before 11pm, and drink more water” start with “I’m going to start exercising with Tony Gonzalez and FitStar.” Making too many changes at once can feel overwhelming, as well as very difficult to sustain. Since we are trying to create new habits, take small steps; don’t try to do everything at once. Implement one small change at a time.

Exercise, rinse, REPEAT
Repeat the behavior you’re aiming for as often as you can. The more a behavior is repeated, the more likely it is that it will become “instinctive.”

It turns out that we are made up of a collection of our habits. If you aren’t thrilled with how you are feeling physically, or how you look in the mirror, examine the collection of habits that have resulted in your appearance –hundreds upon hundreds of actions have drilled these behaviors into your body and mind. Every time you complete an action, a little bit less willpower and brainpower is required to make the same decision. In time, these actions become automatic, and eventually zero brain power is needed. Wake up, turn on FitStar, and off you go!

Additionally, tracking your progress is a great way to reinforce your exercise habit because it gives you a quantifiable way to look back on how successful you’ve been and encourages you to keep pushing further. You can also document your successes by taking progress pictures. They can be every week or every month, but it helps to look back at old pictures of yourself to see how far you have come. Get ready for your “before and after selfies” to track your weekly and monthly gains!

The Result

Reaping the countless rewards of a fitness habit
Creating a fitness habit is so much more than obtaining flatter abs, bigger biceps, and smaller thighs. The benefits (rewards) for creating an exercise habit are many and far reaching.

“In 2002 researches at New Mexico State University wanted to understand why people continued their exercise habit. They studied 266 individuals, most of whom worked out at least 3 times a week. What they found is, the main the reason they continued to habitually exercise was because of a specific reward they started to crave.” -Charles Duhigg

Yes, you will have a stronger, healthier, leaner, sexier body as a result of working out…and so much more. Regular exercise can be rewarding on so many levels. It is well-documented that regular exercise is the one of the best preventative medicines to stave off countless diseases, e.g., heart disease, high blood pressure, diabetes.

In addition, regular exercisers frequently say they have more energy during the day, and sleep better at night. On an emotional level, regular exercise has been shown to relieve stress, help counteract mild depression, and provide a boost in mood by increasing levels of serotonin and norepinephrine, the “soothing” chemicals in our brain.

Regular exercise has been proven to help build self-esteem and self-confidence as well. Exercise definitely contributes to a feeling of empowerment. I felt so empowered by completing that 5K (which I was very unsure I could actually complete) that when I did, It became the catalyst for a lifetime of fitness. The list goes on and on.

So what are you waiting for? Today is a great day to start your fitness habit and start reaping the rewards. Download FitStar on your IPhone or IPad and let Tony and the FitStar crew jump start your fitness journey. Rinse. Repeat. Reward.
