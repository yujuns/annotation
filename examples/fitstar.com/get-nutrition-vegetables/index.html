Get the Most Nutrition from Your Vegetables

By Lori Rice — FitStar Contributor 

Vegetables are the foundation of a healthy eating plan. Think about it. It doesn’t matter what eating style you follow – vegetarian, vegan, Paleo, gluten-free, low carbohydrate.

While these plans may give a yay or nay to fruits, grains, dairy or meat, the one thing they all have in common is vegetables. Veggies are nutrient powerhouses, and as important as eating a variety of vegetables is adding some variety to the how they are prepared.

While heat can decrease vitamin C, B vitamins and some antioxidants, it also enhances other nutrients making it easier for the body to absorb them. Heat also destroys some unwanted substances that lurk in otherwise healthy foods. These are a few of the vegetables I often eat cooked, and the reasons why you might want to consider doing the same.

Broccoli

Raw broccoli maintains its vitamin C levels and cancer-fighting glucosinolates, but don’t be afraid to add it to the steamer. Research shows that steaming broccoli enhances the availability of beta-carotene, and it’s possible that the vitamin C left after cooking is better absorbed than that in raw broccoli. Steaming may also be the best cooking method to reduce heat-related losses of the vitamin and glucosinolates.

Collard Greens, Kale and Mustard Greens  

The ability of food to bind to bile acids leads to cholesterol-lowering potential which reduces risk for cardiovascular disease and some cancers. One study evaluated the bile acid binding ability of cooked versus raw vegetables (including collard greens, kale and mustard greens). Steaming these vegetables improved their ability to bind to the acids boosting their benefit for heart health and their power to protect against disease. 

Mushrooms

Common white button, portobello and cremini mushrooms contain small amounts of the carcinogen agaritine. While it’s unlikely that we would consume enough mushrooms for the agaritine to have a significant effect on health, cooking these mushrooms can reduce the content. One study showed that when cooking methods such as boiling, baking, microwaving and frying were used, the agaritine in the mushrooms was decreased anywhere from 20 to 70 percent.

Spinach

Raw spinach is full of antioxidants, but it also contains more oxalates than other vegetables. Oxalates can interfere with the absorption of calcium. While boiling is not recommended for many vegetables due to nutrient loss, boiling for about one minute is recommended for spinach. This cooking method allows the oxalic acid to leach into the water so that it can be discarded, reducing some of the negative impact on calcium absorption.

Tomatoes

Some argue that a tomato is really a fruit, but it fits in well here with vegetables. Cooking tomatoes boosts their total antioxidant activity. Cooked sauces and salsas, and lightly roasted tomatoes all make it easier for the body to absorb cancer-fighting lycopene.

It’s important to remember that regardless of the vegetable, cooking for extended periods or at high heat can wipe out nutrients. But a little steaming, microwaving, boiling or grilling can bring out some of the best properties vegetables have to offer. So eat fresh green salads, sliced summer tomatoes and pop some kale leaves into your smoothie, but mix in some vegetable stir-fries and steamed greens, too.
