Hydrating for Your Workout

By Lori Rice — FitStar Contributor

Today is World Water Day so what better way to honor life’s elixir than talk about the importance of hydration.

Adequate hydration varies from person to person and it requires some trial and error to find the right delicate balance. The key is to develop a hydration plan that works for you based on health recommendations and your type of training.

Role of Fluids in Exercise
That glistening sweat isn’t just evidence of conquering a tough workout. It plays an important role in your exercise performance. Sweat rids the body of excess heat and when it drips and evaporates away, it takes fluid and electrolytes with it. If these fluids and electrolytes are not replaced, initially exercise performance will suffer. As the dehydration becomes more severe, so do the symptoms and risks, like dizziness, headache and cardiovascular complications.

When and How Much to Drink
A number of factors affect our hydration and sweat loss, which is why the American College of Sports Medicine (ACSM) encourages customized hydration plans. The need for customization became evident to me when training for my first marathon last year. I was running in the same weather conditions, but my fitness level was lower and pace slower than some other participants. All of these factors, along with gender (men tend to sweat more), influence sweat rate and risk of dehydration. I found that regular sips from a water pack kept me well hydrated, while other runners prefer a stop at hydration stations for larger gulps along the way.

Despite vague recommendations to do what works best for you, we all need a starting point. This is what the ACSM recommends as a baseline for fluid intake and exercise:
• 4 hours before exercise – 16 to 20 ounces
• 10 to 15 minutes before exercise – 8 to 12 ounces
• Every 15 to 20 minutes during exercise – 3 to 8 ounces

Rather than using thirst or fatigue to gauge fluid needs, it’s recommended to assess hydration in two ways. First, weigh yourself before and after a workout. The ACSM suggests drinking 20 to 24 ounces of fluid for every pound lost, and to correct this loss within two hours of exercise. Also, pay attention to the percentage of weight lost. If you are losing greater than 2 percent of your body weight, exercise performance decreases. If your loss inches towards 5 percent, you are reaching the danger zone and the serious complications of dehydration.

The second way to estimate hydration is urine color. Darker urine can indicate dehydration, but the goal is not for urine to be clear. The Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics suggests that straw colored urine means you are adequately hydrated while urine that resembles the color of apple juice indicates dehydration.

What to Drink
I’m not a fan of gulping down neon-colored sports drinks on a regular basis, but I do recognize their role in exercise performance. That said, they aren’t fit for every exercise session. For moderate exercise in temperate environments that last less than an hour, water meets the hydration needs of most people.

The ACSM recommends 0.5 to 1 liter of a sports drink with 5 to 8 percent carbohydrate concentration and electrolytes each hour, when exercising at a high intensity for more than 60 minutes. These drinks help to replenish the electrolytes lost through sweat. Something water alone can’t do. Drinking too much water and not replenishing the electrolytes is what leads to hyponatremia, a condition where sodium gets diluted in the body and reaches dangerously low levels.

Lately, I’ve seen a lot of recipes for homemade sports drinks. Being a natural food advocate, I’m a big fan of this idea, but I would give these drinks some trial runs before relying on them for your hydration during intense exercise. Commercially produced drinks have undergone extensive testing in exercise science labs to find the right balance of electrolytes for exercise performance. They also don’t contain fruit juice, but many homemade versions do. The high concentration of fructose in fruit juice has been linked to stomach upset during exercise.

The best way to stay hydrated for your workout is to maintain a healthy hydration level on a regular basis. Keep water handy throughout the day, monitor your weight and urine color, and when your exercise time and intensity decrease electrolytes, replenish them to successfully push through a tough workout.
