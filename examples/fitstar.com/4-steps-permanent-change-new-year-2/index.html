4 Steps to Positive Change in 2014

By Lori Rice — FitStar Contributor

Regardless of whether we call them resolutions or goals, we want our changes to be permanent. No one wants to lose 10 pounds and gain it back. No one aims to do 25 standard push-ups with plans to lose the strength in a month. No one hopes to run their first 5K and be back on the couch a few weeks later.

We want 10 pounds to lead to 15, 25 push-ups to hit 30 and a 5K to result in a 10K down the road. We also know that this kind of change doesn’t happen overnight, and that too much change at once can be detrimental to our success. Yet we wake up on January 1, start a new workout program, a new eating plan, try to get more sleep and spend more time with family.

Failure to succeed has nothing to do with our motivation or willpower and no one can handle so much change at once. Permanent change can only occur if you accomplish a goal and maintain it. What matters isn’t next month, it’s next year.

So, make this the year you change that. Make this the year that you accomplish and maintain your goals with these steps.

Step 1: Pick two habits you want to change

These habits should directly relate to your overall fitness goals. If your goal is to lose weight, these habits should include portion control and exercise. If you want to run a 5K this summer, increasing your running and cross-training sessions is a good place to start. As an example, let’s use healthier eating as an overall goal with the habit of cooking more dinners at home.

Step 2: Determine how to make these changes within the framework of your current lifestyle

This is the part where you have to be realistic, specific and honest. If want to cook more dinners at home, think about how many nights you have time to cook, even if it’s 30 minutes. If you set out to cook five nights a week and you are only home early on Wednesday nights, well, that is setting yourself up for failure. Set a goal to cook two nights per week – one night when you are home early and another night when you prep a slow cooker meal in the morning that will be ready when you get home from work.

Step 3: Maintain that change for one month

Really concentrate on these changes and only these changes. That doesn’t mean you shouldn’t exercise or choose water over soda, but don’t let other changes get in the way of your two original priorities. When we focus our commitment we tend to make other, unexpected improvements. Leftovers from your cooking night may give you the opportunity to eat at home three nights per week. Cooking may motivate you to try new ingredients and increase your fruit and vegetable intake.

Step 4: Select two new habits that build on your success

Once you’ve adopted these two habits as a new part of your lifestyle, it’s time to build on them. But building on them doesn’t mean you abandon them for new goals. As you continue to cook at home two nights per week, you might decide to make it four nights, or that you want to start taking your lunch to work more often. Maybe you are satisfied with your effort to cook and it’s time to attack your soda habit and cut out two per week.

Follow the same steps for your new goals as you maintain what you’ve accomplished. At the rate of two new healthy habits each month you will be well on your way to successful permanent changes in your health and fitness.
